Digital Literacy & Critical Thinking Guide

Learn to question, verify, and think critically about everything you encounter online.

Information Verification Levels

Dangerous: Believing and sharing everything you see without checking
Better: Sometimes questioning suspicious content, checking one source
Good: Regularly fact-checking, cross-referencing multiple sources
Expert: Systematic verification, understanding bias, recognizing manipulation techniques
The SIFT Method
  • Stop: Pause before believing or sharing
  • Investigate the source: Who created this content?
  • Find better coverage: What do other sources say?
  • Trace claims: Follow information back to its origin
Spotting Fake News
  • Check the URL - is it from a reputable source?
  • Look for spelling/grammar errors and poor design
  • Notice emotional, sensational, or extreme language
  • Verify dates - old news presented as current?
  • Check if other credible sources report the same story
Visual Verification
  • Use reverse image search to find original sources
  • Look for signs of photo manipulation or editing
  • Check if images match the story context
  • Verify location and time stamps when possible
  • Be aware that real photos can be used in false contexts
Understanding Bias
  • Recognize that everyone has perspectives and agendas
  • Consider what information might be missing
  • Ask who benefits from you believing this information
  • Seek out opposing viewpoints and different perspectives
  • Be aware of your own confirmation bias
Common Misinformation Tactics
  • Emotional Manipulation: Using fear, anger, or outrage to bypass critical thinking.
  • False Authority: Presenting unqualified people as experts or misrepresenting credentials.
  • Cherry-Picking: Selecting only data that supports a claim while ignoring contradictory evidence.
  • False Urgency: Pressuring you to act or share immediately before you can verify.

Advanced Critical Thinking Strategies

The 5 W's and H Method

Before accepting any information as true, ask yourself:

  • WHO: Who is the source? What are their qualifications and potential biases?
  • WHAT: What exactly is being claimed? Is it specific or vague?
  • WHEN: When was this information published? Is it current or outdated?
  • WHERE: Where did this information originate? Can you trace it back?
  • WHY: Why is this information being shared? What's the purpose or agenda?
  • HOW: How was this information gathered? What's the methodology?
Reliable Source Checklist
  • Author credentials and expertise in the topic
  • Publication date and currency of information
  • Citations and references to primary sources
  • Peer review or editorial oversight
  • Institutional affiliation and reputation
Fact-Checking Resources
  • Snopes.com for urban legends and viral claims
  • FactCheck.org for political statements
  • PolitiFact for political fact-checking
  • Google's fact-check explorer tool
  • Local news stations and established media outlets
Before You Share
  • Have you verified this information yourself?
  • Could sharing this cause harm or spread misinformation?
  • Are you sharing because it's true or because you agree with it?
  • Would you be comfortable if this were proven false later?
Healthy Skepticism
  • Question extraordinary claims that seem too good/bad to be true
  • Look for consensus among experts in relevant fields
  • Distinguish between skepticism and cynicism
  • Be willing to change your mind when presented with evidence

Common Digital Age Myths Debunked

Myth: "If it's trending or viral, it must be true"

Reality: Popularity doesn't equal accuracy. False information often spreads faster than true information because it's more sensational.

Myth: "Screenshots are proof"

Reality: Screenshots can be easily faked, manipulated, or taken out of context. Always verify with original sources.

Myth: "I can trust information from friends and family"

Reality: Well-meaning people can unknowingly share false information. Always verify, regardless of the source.
